# §Voc. Exclamations and direct address[^48]

The vocative is used in exclamations and in direct address: 

→ “[**ὦ**](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=w%29%3D&la=greek&can=w%29%3D0) **[Ζεῦ](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=*zeu%3D&la=greek&can=*zeu%3D0&prior=w\)=)** [καὶ](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=kai%5C&la=greek&can=kai%5C0&prior=*zeu=) [**θεοί**](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=qeoi%2F&la=greek&can=qeoi%2F0&prior=kai\\)” [**P. Pr. 310d**](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Plat.%20Prot.%20310d&lang=original); ⇾ oh Zeus and ye gods

→ “[**ἄνθρωπε**](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=a%29%2Fnqrwpe&la=greek&can=a%29%2Fnqrwpe0&prior=qeoi/)” [**X. C. 2.2.7**](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Xen.%20Cyrop.%202.2.7&lang=original); ⇾ my good fellow 

In ordinary conversation and public speeches, the polite [ὦ](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=w%29%3D&la=greek&can=w%29%3D1&prior=ga/r) is usually added. Without [ὦ](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=w%29%3D&la=greek&can=w%29%3D2&prior=w\)=) the vocative may express astonishment, joy, contempt, a threat, or a warning, etc.:

→ “[ἀκούεις](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=a%29kou%2Feis&la=greek&can=a%29kou%2Feis0&prior=w\)=) [**Αἰσχίνη**](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=*ai%29sxi%2Fnh&la=greek&can=*ai%29sxi%2Fnh0&prior=a\)kou/eis);” [**D. 18.121**](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Dem.%2018.121&lang=original); ⇾ *d'ye hear*, Aeschines? 

But this distinction is not always observed, though in general [ὦ](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=w%29%3D&la=greek&can=w%29%3D3&prior=*ai\)sxi/nh) has a familiar tone which was unsuited to elevated poetry.
