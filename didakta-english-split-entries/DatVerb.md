# §DatVerb. Complement of the verb[^23]

Dative expresses the indirect object of a verb, while the direct object is usually in accusative. The dative may be used as the object of many verbs, such as to benefit, help, injure, please, displease, be friendly or hostile, blame, be angry, threaten, envy, trust, believe, meet, obey, etc.

Object of certain compound verbs (in particular with [σύν](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=su%2Fn&la=greek&can=su%2Fn6&prior=a\)mfisbhtei=n), [ἐν](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=e%29n&la=greek&can=e%29n24&prior=su/n), [ἐπί](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=e%29pi%2F&la=greek&can=e%29pi%2F2&prior=e\)n), and with some compounded with [παρά](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=para%2F&la=greek&can=para%2F1&prior=e\)pi/), [περί](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=peri%2F&la=greek&can=peri%2F1&prior=para/), [πρός](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=pro%2Fs&la=greek&can=pro%2Fs2&prior=peri/), and [ὑπό](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/morph?l=u%28po%2F&la=greek&can=u%28po%2F8&prior=pro/s), etc.) is in dative, because the preposition keeps a sense that requires the dative. The objects in dative often could also be regarded as the dative of advantage, accompaniment, etc.
